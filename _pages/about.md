I am currently a Lecturer in Astronomy/Science Fellow at Columbia University where I split my time between teaching and research responsibilities. As a part of my appointment, I lead two discussion sections of a Columbia College core class, Frontiers of Science, and other introductory classes across Physics and Astronomy. My research primarily focuses on the evolution of galaxies using observations of 21cm neutral hydrogen emission.

Previously, I did my undergraduate studies at Columbia University (2014-2018) where I studied Astrophysics, Mathematics, and Philosophy, and conducted research with Professor Jacqueline van Gorkom as a part of the CHILES collaboration. Following that, I did my doctoral studies at West Virginia University (2018-2022) where I continued work with CHILES under the advising of Professor DJ Pisano. and completed my thesis, Deep Radio Observations and the Role of the Cosmic Web in Galaxy Evolution.

In my free time, I enjoy discovering new corners of New York City, unwinding at local wineries, getting lost in Wikipedia rabbit holes, and spending time on the water sailing.

Whether it’s to chat about astronomy, teaching, potential collaborations, or just to say hello, I’d love to hear from you at nicholas.m.luber@gmail.com

    <h2>Tiny Titans - HI</h2>
    <p>I study dwarf–dwarf galaxy interactions through the TiNy Titans HI survey, which explores how the smallest galaxies affect one another’s evolution. As part of this project, we have collected more than 300 hours of VLA observations for both neutral hydrogen and radio continuum, targeting over a dozen interacting systems. Beyond characterizing the dynamics of galaxy pairs, our work has revealed new insights into the behavior of low-mass galaxy groups and uncovered a candidate case of ram-pressure stripping driven by the cosmic web.</p>
