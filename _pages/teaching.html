I strive to foster active participation and scientific curiosity in my classrooms, connecting core concepts to real-world applications while inspiring a sense of wonder. My courses emphasize problem solving and collaborative engagement, encouraging students to explore, question, and discover for themselves.

As a Lecturer in Astronomy, I have gained a wide range of teaching experience, from leading small, discussion-based seminars of 10 students to instructing large introductory courses with over 140 students. Across all formats, my goal is to make science tangible by fostering participation, connecting concepts to real-world applications, and cultivating enthusiasm for discovery.

I have served as a discussion instructor for Frontiers of Science for six semesters, guiding groups of 10–22 students in applying scientific habits of mind to the material presented in large lectures. I have also designed and taught Life in the Universe, an introductory astronomy course that uses the Drake Equation as a framework for exploring the fundamental concepts of astronomy. In addition, I have taught General Physics I (Mechanics) and General Physics II (Electricity & Magnetism), algebra-based courses that serve as required introductory physics for pre-medical students.
