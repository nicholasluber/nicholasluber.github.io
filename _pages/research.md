My research focuses on how galaxies acquire, lose, and process their gas across cosmic time. I use radio, millimeter, and optical observations to study the physical mechanisms that regulate star formation, especially in low-mass and environmentally influenced galaxies.

A major component of my work involves characterizing gas-rich systems on the verge of quenching, including galaxies embedded within filaments of the cosmic web. I am particularly interested in how interactions with the intergalactic medium remove or redistribute gas, and how those processes may suppress star formation before traditional environmental mechanisms become effective.

I work extensively with ALMA, the VLA, and wide-field optical surveys to measure neutral and molecular gas content, dynamical structure, and low-surface-brightness emission in and around galaxies. I also develop analysis techniques for time-variable sources and for detecting weak signals buried in observational noise.

Recent and ongoing projects include:

* studying cosmic-web stripping in low-mass, non–star-forming galaxies

* using high-resolution interferometry to probe time-variable radio emission

* characterizing extended H I reservoirs and faint gas streams in nearby systems

* developing machine-learning approaches to improve detection and classification of diffuse structures

My long-term goal is to connect gas dynamics on sub-galactic scales to the larger-scale cosmic environment, linking individual galaxies to the filamentary structure they inhabit.
